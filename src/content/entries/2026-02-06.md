Some mornings the news feels like the Carthaginians at the gates. Lucretius, writing in the first century BCE, evokes a Rome gripped by the terror of invasion, when "all things, shuddering, were filled / With tumult of the shaken firmament" and no one knew whose dominion would remain when the dust settled. We know this vertigo. It arrives in different uniforms—climate reports, midnight anxieties, the slow unraveling of certainties we mistook for bedrock—but the physiological response is the same: the tightened chest, the scanning of horizons.

What Lucretius offers is not reassurance that the walls will hold, but a stranger comfort. He asks us to remember that we have already survived the worst possible catastrophe. Before the particular constellation of atoms that is each of us came together, there was a silence identical to the one that follows death. We did not suffer then. We were not deprived of anything, because there was no "we" to be deprived. The universe got along perfectly well, rearranging itself into seas and stars and eventually into the temporary pattern reading these words.

This is not nihilism; it is a radical intimacy with existence. To know that non-being is not a punishment but simply the other side of the coin—and one we have already flipped—frees us to occupy this side with less clutching. The atoms that trembled during the Punic Wars are the same ones composing the coffee cup and the hands around it. We are, Lucretius suggests, a brief gathering of that which has always been, returning soon to the dance. The Carthaginians are not coming. They were never the point. The point is that we are here now, while the firmament holds.
